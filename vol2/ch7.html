Volume II Chapter Seven

Marriage & The New Life

Age 30 · Antipolo Garden · November 2036
NOVEMBER 2036 · ANTIPOLO

Forty-one people. A garden in Antipolo with the kind of November light that happens only in the months after the rains, when the air has been washed and the mountains to the east are clear and the candles Yuna's mother helped arrange along the aisle make the whole space smell faintly of warmth. It is not a large wedding. It was never going to be a large wedding. They chose the size the same way they choose most things: by asking what they actually want rather than what the occasion expects.

Pastor Reyes officiates. This was decided before the venue, before the flowers, before the date. He is the first call Haruki made after Yuna said yes, and when the pastor said yes in return, he said it with the specific warmth of someone who has been expecting this call since a Wednesday prayer meeting when a sixteen-year-old played piano for an audience of fifteen.

· · ·
THE VOWS

He writes his at 3 AM the night before. Not because he left them too late — he has been writing them in his head for a year — but because some things only come clear in the dark, when the city is quiet and the page is blank and there is no performance available to hide behind.

"You saw me at the piano before school did. You stayed through Saudi Arabia. You chose faith in a person the same way you choose faith in God — not because you can see the outcome, but because you know the character. I will spend whatever comes next being worthy of that. I will be difficult sometimes. I will be at the piano when I should be sleeping. I will wake at 5 AM. I will make decisions that seem long-term when you want them short-term. I will have plans within plans and I will not always explain them clearly the first time. I promise to always come back. I will always, always come back to you."

She memorized hers. Nursing trained her to function from memory in high-stakes situations, and this is the highest-stakes situation she has ever entered by choice, and she does not want paper between her face and his.

"Haruki. I watched you play in a church when you thought no one important was watching. I thought: that is a man who gives his best when there is no audience. I want to be married to that man for the rest of my life."

Forty-one people, some of whom know the full story and some of whom know only fragments, are entirely still during both.

Marco, in the second row, has to press his lips together to maintain composure, which he does not entirely succeed at.

· · ·

Pastor Reyes pronounces them husband and wife. He does this with the specific gravity of a man who has been present at the beginning of this story and is now witnessing what the beginning was for. He holds the moment one beat longer than strictly required — long enough for it to be real, not so long that it becomes performance.

· · ·
THE FIRST DANCE

Their graduation song.

Forty-one people who know or don't know the history — it doesn't matter. The song is the story. It is the story of a high school auditorium and a piano facing slightly wrong and a girl in the middle section of the hall with her hands folded in her lap. It is the story of a courtyard at night and the most important line of Volume I: That's why I stayed.

They dance to it in the November garden in Antipolo. The candles are still lit. The mountains are still clear. The forty-one people watch two people who have been building toward this evening since a Tuesday night in Proverbs 21:5, and the feeling in the garden is the feeling of a thing that was always going to happen arriving in its correct time.

His mother is crying. Her mother is not — she is watching with the specific composed attention of a woman who has revised her assessment of a person and has been proven right to revise it, and she is satisfied in the deepest and most private way.

Lola Perla, who made the trip from the church barangay and who is eighty-three years old and does not travel easily, is in the front row of the guest section, sitting very straight, watching them dance with the expression of someone who has been watching for a long time and finds the conclusion correct.

It is November. The mountains are visible. She is here. I am here. The pastor who told me all stages are sacred when the heart behind them is has just pronounced us married. Every 5 AM was for this. Every Saturday TESDA module was for this. Every bad night in Riyadh with the letter in my wallet was for this. Not this specific moment — this moment is too good to have been planned for. But this: the condition of having something real, with a person who is real, in a life that is becoming what it was always meant to be.

The song ends. The garden applauds. He dips her — not dramatically, not as a performance, but because the song is over and the moment calls for it and he has learned, in thirty years of living, to give moments what they call for.

She laughs. It is the full laugh, the sustained one. The one that means something is genuinely good.

He straightens. He holds her hand. They face forty-one people who love them, in a November garden, in the year 2036, in the country where they grew up and chose to stay.
