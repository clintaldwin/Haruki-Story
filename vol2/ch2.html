Volume II Chapter Two — Phase 3

The Dual-Track Education

Age 18–22 · BS IT · 2026–2030
2026 · UNIVERSITY ENROLLMENT

He chooses BS Information Technology with the same deliberateness he has brought to every decision since the Tuesday night in Proverbs. Not because everyone is doing it. Not because it is safe. Because he has thought about the landscape of the next decade and arrived at a conclusion he is willing to stake several years of his life on.

THE REASONING AI will displace 35% of entry-level jobs within a decade. Common response: fear, avoidance, denial. Correct response: become the person who builds and directs these systems. IT is not the escape from the technological shift. IT is the command post. "Hindi ako mag-IT para takasan ang AI. Mag-IT ako para pangunahan siya." — I'm not taking IT to run from AI. I'm taking IT to lead it.
Programming Systems Architecture Networks Databases Financial Literacy Side Hustles

He sits in the front row. This is new — he was a back-row person for the first year of high school, and the shift to the front is not about visibility but about information. He has learned that proximity to the source matters. He takes notes in the same color-coded system that has been running since Proverbs 21:5.

The professors are, for the most part, competent and occasionally excellent. He treats them the way he treated Mrs. Andres — as sources of information to be fully extracted, not as authorities to be pleased. He is polite. He asks specific questions. He does the work.

His side hustle begins in the second semester: part-time merchandiser on weekends, and small freelance Python scripts for local small businesses that need automation and don't know it yet. He charges modestly. He overdelivers. Three of his first five clients refer him to someone else.

THE LONG-DISTANCE ARCHITECTURE

Yuna is in Manila. He is two hours away by bus. This is a fact, not a complaint.

They call every night. Sometimes for two minutes — she has just come off a twelve-hour clinical shift and her voice has the particular flatness of someone who has been fully present for other people all day and has nothing left for performance, which means what she gives him is real. Sometimes for two hours, when neither can sleep and the city is quiet and they talk about everything and nothing in the specific freedom of two people who have stopped being careful around each other.

The distance is real. It is present every morning when he wakes and the first instinct is to tell her something and then remembers the geography. But it does not break them because they are both in the middle of becoming, and they can feel it in each other's voices — a steadiness that is not performed confidence but actual substance being built, day by day, at 5 AM and in twelve-hour clinical shifts and in front-row lecture halls.

She sounds different on the phone than she did at school. Not different from herself — more herself. The nursing is working on her the way the piano has always worked on me: not adding something foreign but revealing what was already there. She is becoming the thing she decided to become. I can hear it. It is the best sound in the world.

Plot Twist #8 — The Faith Test

Second year of college. Evolutionary psychology lecture. The professor — brilliant, specific, accustomed to being the most interesting mind in the room — makes a comment that is not quite a dismissal of religious belief but contains one inside a parenthesis. The class absorbs it with the passive acceptance of students who have not yet been asked to disagree with a professor on a matter of substance.

A student named Baguio, who knows Haruki's reputation as the top of their program, turns to him: "Haruki, ikaw yung matalino dito — talagang naniniwala ka pa rin sa Diyos?"

Haruki, you're the smart one here — do you actually still believe in God?

The class waits. This is the kind of moment that a room anticipates — the kind where the answer will define something.

Haruki puts down his pen. He looks at Baguio with the complete calm of someone who has held this faith since he was fourteen, who played for God in an empty church when no one was watching, who has never separated his intelligence from his belief because he has never found a reason to.

"Yes. And the existence of intelligence — mine, yours, the professor's — is actually one of the reasons I do."

The room goes quiet. Not hostile. Not converted. Just — surprised. The specific surprise of a room that expected a defensive retreat and received instead a completely settled advance.

The professor, from the front of the room — who has been listening — says: "That's a conversation worth having after class."

They have it. The professor is not converted. Haruki does not expect him to be. But he earns the professor's quiet respect — not for the content of his belief, but for the quality of his certainty. A person who knows what they think and can hold it in the open, without anxiety or aggression, is a person worth having a conversation with.

Faith is not my private corner. It is my backbone. It goes with me into every room I enter, not because I carry it as a shield but because it is structural — it is load-bearing. Remove it and the shape of my life changes. This is not a problem to solve. It is simply the truth of how I am built.

He calls Yuna that night and tells her. She listens. Then: "Siyempre ganyan ang sagot mo." Of course that was your answer. It is the highest compliment she knows how to give: the recognition that he is exactly who she thought he was.
