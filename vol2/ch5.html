Volume II Chapter Five

The Reunion & Yuna RN

Age 28 · NAIA · March 2034
NAIA ARRIVALS · MARCH 2034

He lands at noon. March. The Manila heat is immediate and specific — not the dry heat of Riyadh, which has its own clarity, but the tropical density of a city built on a delta, warm and layered and smelling of rain that hasn't arrived yet. He steps through the arrivals gate carrying a duffel bag and four years of accumulated purpose.

His parents are there. His mother reaches him first — she always reaches people first, which is one of the things he has always loved about her — and she holds him the way she held him at the airport four years ago in reverse, except now there is no grief of departure, only the specific joy of a return. He lets himself be held. He is twenty-eight years old and not too old for this.

His father's handshake: firm, held one beat past its natural end. The handshake says everything his father does not say, which is considerable.

Then Yuna.

She is standing behind his parents. She gave them their moment — this is one of the specific ways she has always shown love, by understanding what a moment belongs to and not crowding it. She is in scrubs. She came directly from a shift. Her hair is pinned in the way she pins it for twelve-hour days on the ward, and she is standing with the posture of someone who has spent four years doing difficult work that she chose and does not regret, and she looks like someone who has become, quietly and completely, what she set out to become.

"Welcome back," she says.

"Nandito na." I'm here now.

He crosses the space between them. He holds her the way you hold someone you have been holding in your mind for four years — completely, without reservation, with the specific relief of a thing finally made physical after being abstract for too long.

She holds back with equal completeness.

· · ·
ON YUNA · THE NURSE SHE BECAME

This is what four years of nursing has given her, and it is not a résumé:

The ability to be present in the hardest moments without flinching. The specific skill of seeing a person clearly when they are at their most frightened and most vulnerable — not past their fear, through it, with the calm acknowledgment of someone who has been trained to make the terrifying ordinary enough to be managed. She does not minimize. She does not perform comfort. She simply stays, and knowing she will stay changes everything.

This is what her brother's death made of her: a person who stays.

She is good at her work in the way that has nothing to do with recognition — in the way of someone who chose a ward she believed in and a hospital she trusted and a schedule that challenged her rather than destroyed her. She has colleagues who respect her. She has patients who remember her after discharge. She has the particular authority of a person who has earned their competence in full.

And within a few years she will work part-time, by choice, because the blueprint will have made it possible. This is freedom: not the absence of work, but the presence of choice. She is better at nursing now, in three shifts a week, than she was in five — because she arrives rested, because she arrives having chosen it rather than surviving it, because choice changes what work feels like from the inside.

· · ·
Plot Twist #10 — Yuna's Mother Comes Around

The family dinner is two weeks after his return. Her mother cooks — an elaborate spread, which means something, because elaborate food is the language her family uses for occasions that matter.

Her mother has been watching Haruki for four years from a distance. She watched him leave. She watched the remittances arrive for his parents on schedule. She watched Yuna's voice during calls from Riyadh become steadier rather than less steady. She watched him send Yuna a book every two months — not expensive, never expensive, but always specific, always the right thing for where she was in her studies or her thinking, which means he had been paying attention from six thousand kilometers away.

After dinner, she takes him aside. Not dramatically. She finds him in the kitchen when he goes to refill the water jug, and she speaks quietly.

"Haruki. Sorry sa dati."

He looks at her. He sees a woman who has been carrying the same protective love for her daughter that his own mother has been carrying for him — differently expressed, sometimes wrongly expressed, but structurally the same.

"Wala pong problema, Ma," he says. There's no problem, Ma. "Kayo pa rin ang magulang niya." You are still her parent.

She nods once. It is a specific nod — the nod of a parent who has assessed a person over time and concluded the assessment. From this evening forward, she is fully on his side, in the way of Filipino mothers who have decided: completely, practically, and without reservation.

She adds extra rice to his plate for the rest of the meal. This is the language.

· · ·

He drives home that night through the city. The Manila traffic is the same — dense, lit, indifferent to individuals in the way of all large cities. He has been gone four years. The city has not waited for him, which is correct — cities don't wait for anyone — but he is back now, and the blueprint is entering its final phases, and somewhere in the passenger seat of his father's old car, he can feel the pieces beginning to assemble into the thing they were always meant to be.

She is here. I am here. We have both become something. Not finished — finished is for projects, not people — but substantially, verifiably ourselves. The rest is architecture. The rest is what the 5 AMs were for.
