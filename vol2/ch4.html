Volume II Chapter Four — Phase 4

Saudi Arabia — Capital Extraction

Age 24–28 · Riyadh · 2030–2034
2030 · THE OFFER

The offer comes through his uncle — who works in logistics in Riyadh and has been watching Haruki's trajectory with the particular attention of a relative who has always suspected something — and it arrives in the form of a phone call on a Tuesday afternoon between his last university exam and the graduation ceremony two weeks away.

Systems and Cloud Infrastructure Engineer. A mid-sized tech company servicing the Gulf's construction and logistics sector. Tax-free. Competitive salary in riyals. Housing allowance. Three-year contract, renewable.

He prepares for the interview for three weeks. Not because he is unqualified but because preparation is the difference between deserving something and getting it, and he has never confused the two. He studies the company's infrastructure stack, their client list, the specific problems they are likely trying to solve. He goes in knowing more about their systems than they expected.

He gets the role.

THE AIRPORT · THE DEPARTURE

She is there. This is the first fact.

She has worked a half-shift and come directly from the hospital — she is in scrubs, her hair pinned, her shoes the comfortable ones she wears for twelve-hour shifts on her feet. She looks like what she is: a person who has spent the morning keeping other people alive and is now here because here is where she needs to be.

His parents are there. His mother holds him for a long time. His father's handshake is the firm kind, held one beat past its natural end — the handshake of a father releasing a son he is proud of and does not know how else to say it.

Then Yuna. She gives his parents their moment. She waits, standing slightly apart, with the composure she has developed across four years of nursing — the composure of someone who knows that the most important thing she can do in a high-emotion situation is remain steady.

At the gate, he holds her for a long time. Neither of them says anything immediately. He can smell the hospital on her scrubs — antiseptic and something warmer underneath — and she is warm and real and present, and the gate is ahead, and the four years of distance is ahead, and none of this is easy.

"Haruki — babalik ka, di ba?"

Haruki — you're coming back, right?

He pulls back enough to see her. She is looking at him with the particular look she uses for things she takes seriously — direct, fully there, no performance.

"Babalik ako," he says. "At hindi lang basta babalik. Babalik akong may dala."

I'll come back. And not just come back. I'll come back carrying something.

She holds his gaze for a moment. Then nods once — the serious nod, the one that means she believes him not because she wants to but because the evidence supports it.

He goes through the gate.

RIYADH · THE YEARS

The work is real and demanding in the way of work that matters. He is younger than most of his team and more competent than several of them — not because of arrogance but because of the dual-track education, because he understands physical systems in a way that software-only engineers do not, because he has spent four years building the habit of full engagement and brought it with him across the Arabian Sea.

His NC II certifications surface quietly and usefully. When a server room has a wiring problem, he can diagnose it physically. When the solar array on their company compound needs assessment, he is the one who goes up. When the kitchen at the staff housing needs a rewire, he does it on a Saturday morning and charges nothing. These things build a specific kind of reputation — the kind that comes from being useful in ways that were not in the job description.

He finds a Filipino Christian community in Riyadh by his third week. They meet on Sunday afternoons in an apartment in the Al Olaya district — forty-odd people from the Philippines, a mix of nurses, engineers, domestic workers, teachers. They worship quietly, carefully, in the particular way of people who are doing something they love in a place that requires them to be discreet about it.

He plays keyboard for their Sunday gatherings. He does not have the upright piano. He has a sixty-one-key portable keyboard he bought in his second month, which he plays at the lowest possible volume in his apartment when he needs the language and at the full warm volume of corporate worship when the community gathers. He leads the worship set the same way he always has — fully, from the place that is not performance.

Some things are non-negotiable regardless of geography.

· · ·

He misses her. This is honest and present, not managed or rationalized. He misses the library Thursday silence. He misses her voice at its full register rather than the compressed version that arrives through a phone speaker at eleven PM his time, two in the morning hers. He misses her in the specific way of someone who has never tried to minimize what she means to him and does not start now.

He carries it the way he carries everything: fully, without letting it stop him.

Plot Twist #9 — The Letter

In the middle of his second year, a letter arrives. Actual paper. Handwritten. His name on the envelope in the careful cursive of someone who has been writing by hand since before ballpoints were common.

It is from Pastor Reyes.

He opens it carefully, the way you open things you suspect you will want to keep. The paper is slightly thick, the kind that comes from a pad bought for correspondence rather than notes. The handwriting is unhurried.

Haruki,

Ang isang bata na tumutugtog ng piano para sa Panginoon nang walang nanonood ay siyang magiging lalaking magtatayo ng entablado para sa kanyang pamilya.

Patuloy ka. Nakikita ka pa rin.

— Ptr. Reyes

The boy who played piano for the Lord when no one was watching will become the man who builds the stage for his family. Keep going. You are still seen.

He folds the letter. He puts it in his wallet — the old brown wallet his father gave him before he left, the kind with the stitching starting to loosen at the top left corner.

He reads it on bad nights. Not every bad night — not as a ritual or a crutch — but on the specific nights when the distance is heaviest and the savings tracker shows good numbers that feel abstract and Riyadh is beautiful in its alien way and the piano in his apartment is sixty-one keys instead of eighty-eight and he is twenty-five years old and far from every person whose name he knows.

Nakikita ka pa rin. You are still seen.

He folds the letter back. He puts it back. He sleeps.

In the morning: 5 AM. The body remembers. The compass points the same direction it always has.
