The TESDA Grind

Age 18–22 · Summers, Breaks, Weekends
THE PHILOSOPHY

While his classmates use semestral breaks for rest — which is legitimate — and summer for travel — which is also legitimate — Haruki uses them for certificates. This is not a judgment about rest. Rest is structural; he builds it into his week the same way he builds the 5 AM and the soul block. But breaks are time, and time is the one resource that cannot be recovered, and there are things that can only be learned in specific environments that only exist outside a university classroom.

THE TESDA STACK (1–2 courses per year, breaks and summers) 1. Computer Systems Servicing NC II → Hardware synergy with IT degree. Software engineers see abstraction. You need to see the machine. 2. Photovoltaic Systems Installation NC II → For the farm. Solar array, self-installed. The land should power itself. 3. Electrical Installation & Maintenance NC II → Wire your own buildings. Call no one for what you can do yourself. 4. Cookery NC II → The restaurant needs someone who understands food, not just finance. Your mother's recipes deserve a system. 5. Animal Production NC II → Poultry. Swine. The farm foundation. Optional but not unimportant. THE PRINCIPLE: Software tells machines what to do. Hardware knowledge makes you irreplaceable. Skills make you free.
CSS NC II PV Systems NC II EIM NC II Cookery NC II Animal Prod NC II

He takes the Computer Systems Servicing course first because it is adjacent to what he is already studying and because he wants to understand what his code runs on. The theoretical becomes tactile. He dismantles a motherboard. He reassembles it. He recognizes in the physical architecture the same logic he has been building in Python — hierarchies, dependencies, the beautiful economy of systems that do exactly what they are designed to do and nothing more.

The Photovoltaic Systems course comes the following summer, because the farm is a plan and the plan needs a power source, and the most honest thing a person can do with a plan is begin working on its infrastructure before the plan is fully funded. He learns the physics of solar conversion, the math of load calculation, the specific language of wiring diagrams. He takes notes in the color-coded system. He installs a test array on his parents' roof.

His father watches him on the roof one Saturday morning with the particular expression of a parent who is watching their child exceed them and feeling only pride about it. He doesn't say anything. He hands up a bottle of water when Haruki comes down. This is enough.

THE YUNA VISIT · THE ANIMAL HUSBANDRY MOMENT

She visits during the Christmas break of his third year. She comes from Manila with the particular quality of someone who has spent months doing difficult work in high-stakes environments and is now briefly, gratefully, somewhere smaller and quieter. She is more herself than she was before nursing school — or rather, she is the same self but with more of the unnecessary parts stripped away.

She comes to his room to call him for dinner and finds him at his desk with two books open side by side. The Python textbook — expected. And an animal husbandry module, its cover featuring a photograph of pigs in a clean pen, which is not expected.

She stares at it.

"Haruki."

He looks up with the serenity of a person who sees no inconsistency.

"Baboy?" she says. Pigs?

"Pangmatagalang plano." Long-term plan.

She looks at him for a moment. Then she laughs — a real laugh, the sustained kind, the kind that happens when something is genuinely, specifically funny rather than merely socially appropriate. Thirty seconds of it. He watches her with the expression of someone who has been waiting for this exact reaction and finds it exactly as good as anticipated.

When she finishes, she sits down beside him. She picks up the animal husbandry module. She reads the first page.

"Okay," she says. "Ituro mo sa akin." Teach me.

He does. They spend the next hour and a half reading together — she with the module, he with the Python book, the same library silence they have been practicing since high school, still available, still good, still the most natural thing in the world.

She did not ask me to explain the pig plan. She did not need it explained. She sat down and read the module. This is the specific intimacy of two people who accept each other's entire vision without needing it translated. Whatever I am building, she has decided to understand it. That is not a small thing.

At dinner, she tells his mother about the animal husbandry module. His mother laughs too. His father nods with the expression of a man who has been expecting this kind of news for some time and is not surprised.

The family table, warm and ordinary and full.
