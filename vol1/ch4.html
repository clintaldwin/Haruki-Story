Volume I Chapter Four

The Multi-Talented Mind

Age 16–17 · Month 4
BREAK TIME · CLASSROOM 2-C

He forgets to hide the Rubik's cube on a Thursday morning break. This is the correct way to describe it — not he decides to show it, not he chooses this moment for revelation. He simply forgets that hiding it has ever been his default, because he has been doing a lot of things differently lately and the habit of invisibility is losing its grip.

He takes it out because his hands need something to do while he reviews his notes. He solves it in the way you do something you have done so many times that the conscious mind can do other things simultaneously — his eyes on his notes, his hands moving through the algorithm with the same practiced certainty as a musician running scales.

Forty-eight seconds.

He becomes aware of the watching by the thirty-second mark but doesn't stop. Six people are standing nearby — he clocks them peripherally without turning his head. He finishes the solve. He places the solved cube on his desk. He returns to his notes.

He does not say anything. He does not look up. He does not perform the moment.

Berto says, after a long beat: "Pre. Sinabi mo ba na nagpipiano ka? At ngayon ito pa?" Man. You said you played piano? And now this too?

"Matagal ko na 'yan," Haruki says. I've been doing that a long time.

He goes back to his notes.

· · ·
THE CHESS TOURNAMENT

He enters the school chess tournament without announcement. His name appears on the signup sheet between two students who are known chess players, and when Marcus sees it during registration he raises an eyebrow but says nothing, because Marcus has now been paying attention to Haruki long enough to know that Haruki's quiet entries into things tend to become interesting.

He advances through the bracket methodically. He does not rush. He does not perform concentration — he simply concentrates, in the way of someone who has taught himself to be fully present in whatever he is doing, who understands that the mind is a muscle you can train and that full attention is its full engagement.

He beats the semifinalist from 3-B in fourteen moves. He is in the final before lunch.

The final is against Dela Cruz. Third year. Defending champion for two consecutive years. He has the particular bearing of someone who has been excellent at one thing for a very long time and has organized his identity around it — not arrogantly, but with the settled confidence of a person who knows what they are.

They play for thirty-seven minutes. Haruki studies the board the way he studied his classmates' patterns of attention — observationally, structurally, looking not for the obvious move but for the logic of the position. He finds it. He plays it.

Dela Cruz tips his king on move thirty-one.

The room — there are perhaps twenty spectators by this point — is quiet in the way of rooms that have just witnessed something they did not quite expect. Not upset. Not celebratory. Just — recalibrating.

Dela Cruz extends his hand across the board. He shakes it the way good athletes shake hands — fully, without reservation, with the specific respect of one skilled player for another.

"Ikaw yung church piano guy, di ba?" he says. You're the church piano guy, right?

"Oo."

"Yun ang pinakamagandang laro na nilaro ko ng dalawang taon." That was the best game I've played in two years.

The church piano guy. The chess winner. The quiz topper. The piano at the CICF conference. They are all the same person. In Dela Cruz's eyes, they are not separate things to reconcile — they are just me. It is strange and right to hear myself assembled that way by someone from the outside.

SUNDAY · AFTER SERVICE
Plot Twist #3 — The Pastor's Challenge

Pastor Reyes finds him in the sound room after Sunday service, where Haruki is coiling cables with the particular care of someone who respects the equipment they use. The pastor does not announce himself. He leans in the doorway and waits until Haruki becomes aware of him.

"Narinig ko mula kay Marco," he says. I heard from Marco.

Haruki straightens. "Ano po ang narinig ninyo?" What did you hear?

"Na nag-iba ka. Sa school. Ang galing mo na ngayon sa klase, na nanalo ka sa chess, na nagko-code ka."

He says it not as praise, exactly — or not only as praise. He says it as information he has received and is now examining. He is looking at Haruki the way he looks at things he considers important: with his full attention, which is considerable.

"Haruki," he says. "Ang talento mo sa piano ay para sa Diyos. Pero ang isipan mo, ang disiplina mo — 'yon din. Huwag i-bury ang kahit isa."

Haruki. Your gift for piano is for God. But your mind, your discipline — those too. Don't bury either one.

Haruki holds the cable he has been coiling. The sanctuary outside is emptying — he can hear families filing past the doorway, the usual after-service conversation, children.

"Lahat po?" he says. All of it?

"Lahat," says Pastor Reyes. All of it. "Ang buhay mo ay hindi na-divide sa 'church Haruki' at 'school Haruki.' Isa ka lang. Ibigay mo lahat — doon, dito, at kahit saan pa."

Your life is not divided into 'church Haruki' and 'school Haruki.' You are one person. Give all of it — there, here, and wherever else.

All stages are sacred when the heart behind them is. I did not have those words before tonight. Now I do. They will be the architecture of whatever comes next.

THE ICT FAIR

He submits his algorithm project to the school's ICT fair three weeks later. It is a study schedule optimizer built on spaced repetition and retention curve data — practical, specific, the kind of thing that has grown from his own system but could be used by anyone. He spends four days on it. He does not tell anyone.

It places second.

His name appears on the bulletin board outside the ICT room: 2nd Place — Aizawa, Haruki, 2-C.

He photographs it with his phone. He sends it to one person: Pastor Reyes. The caption is: Dini-diply ko na po. I'm deploying it now.

The pastor replies within a minute: a single emoji. A small flame.

Haruki looks at it for a moment. He puts his phone away. He goes back to class.
