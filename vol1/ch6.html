Volume I Chapter Six

The Girl

Age 17 · Library · Thursday
THE LIBRARY · THURSDAY AFTERNOON

It begins with a study table. The library is not full on Thursday afternoons — this is why they both use it, she has come to realize, though she does not yet know that he has come to the same realization about her. They are both the kind of people who know when a room is optimal and return to it.

She is already at the table when he arrives. She does not look up when he sits. He does not announce his arrival. This is the first Thursday this has happened and also, looking back on it later, obviously not the first time they have shared this space without noticing.

They read in the same silence for forty minutes.

It is not an uncomfortable silence. It is the specific comfortable silence of two people who do not need to fill space with sound to prove that something is happening. It is the silence of a reading room. It is good.

On the forty-first minute, she says, without looking up from her notes: "Ang edition mo ng textbook, maling chapter ang coverage para sa next exam."

Your edition of the textbook has the wrong chapter coverage for the next exam.

He looks at his textbook. He looks at hers. She is correct. He has the third edition; the exam covers content that was reorganized in the fourth. He makes a note. He says: "Sa'yo rin — ang third chapter ng fourth edition ay mali ang sequence ng mga formula."

Yours too — the third chapter in the fourth edition has the formula sequence wrong.

She looks up for the first time. It is the same assessing look from the day with the novel, measuring, quick, and then: "Talaga?" Really?

"Erratum na-publish nila noong December." They published an erratum in December.

She looks at him for another beat. Then she opens her notebook and writes something. Then she goes back to reading.

They read for another hour.

· · ·

The Thursdays become a pattern without anyone deciding they would be. She is there when he arrives, or he is there when she arrives, and neither of them remarks on this because remarking on it would make it something other than what it simply is: two people who use the library on Thursdays.

They talk more as the weeks pass. Not constantly — they are both, at their core, people who do not talk for the sake of filling silence. But when they talk, it is direct and specific. She has opinions about everything and does not preface them with apology. He respects this. He has the same quality, though it expresses itself differently in him — quieter in delivery, equally precise in content.

"Anong course ang gusto mo pagkatapos ng high school?" she asks one Thursday, not looking up.

"IT," he says. "Pero may plano pa pagkatapos." But there's a plan beyond that.

"Anong klase ng plano?" What kind of plan?

He is quiet for a moment. "Malaki," he says. A big one.

She looks up. "Nursing ako," she says. Not I want to take nursing. Not I'm thinking about nursing. I am nursing. As if the decision has already been made so completely that the present tense is the only accurate one.

THE QUESTION

On a Thursday in late September, the light through the library windows is amber and long, and she closes her book and says, without preamble:

"Bakit ka nagchurch? Like, talagang naniniwala ka?"

Why do you go to church? Like, do you actually believe?

It is not a hostile question. It is the question of someone who is genuinely curious, who has been thinking about it — he can tell this from the directness, the way it comes out with no wrapper of apology or softening. She does not ask things she hasn't thought about first.

He is quiet. Not because he doesn't have an answer but because the answer matters and he wants to say it correctly. He has been playing piano at church since he was fourteen. He has led worship for two years. He has held this faith in private for most of his life. Now it is being asked about directly, in good faith, by someone worth being honest with.

"Kasi noong pinaka-hindi ako nakikita ng lahat — nakikita niya," he says.

Because when no one else saw me — He did.

She looks at him. The amber light is between them.

It is not the answer she expected. She expected something doctrinal, or something performative, or something that could be argued with. This is none of those things. This is simply honest — the kind of honest that is harder to answer than a theological position because it is not a claim about the universe but a statement about a person's experience of it.

She does not say anything for a long moment. She looks at the table.

She comes back to the church the following Sunday. And the Sunday after that.

Plot Twist #5 — Yuna's Hidden Wound

She tells him about her brother on a Thursday in October. Not immediately, not all at once — it comes in pieces, the way things come that have been carried quietly for a long time and are finally finding an exit that feels safe.

His name was Kenji. He was eleven. He had a fever that became a complication that became a hospitalization that became, over the course of nine days, something her family could not stop. He died three years ago. A nursing error, her family believes — never proven, never pursued, because proving things requires money and time and a kind of sustained anger that her parents could not maintain while also grieving.

She does not say this with heat. She says it the way she says everything — directly, precisely, without performance. This is the most devastating thing about it: how long she has been carrying this in the same even tone she uses to describe everything else.

"Kaya nursing," she says. That's why nursing. "Hindi ambisyon. Kasi may mali na nangyari, at ayoko na mangyari 'yon ulit. Sa akin na bahagi. Sa kahit sino."

Not ambition. Because something went wrong, and I don't want it to happen again. In my part. To anyone.

Haruki is very still. He does not move to comfort immediately — he has learned, from music, that there are moments that require witnessing before they require response. He lets the words be in the room. He lets her be in them.

Then he says: "Ibig sabihin, magiging extraordinary ka. Hindi kasi career 'yan para sa'yo. Ginagawa mo 'yan para sa kanya."

That means you're going to be extraordinary. Because it's not a career for you. You're doing it for him.

She looks at the table. A long moment.

She cries quietly. Not loudly, not dramatically — the specific quiet crying of someone who is not used to crying in front of people and is doing it anyway because something has been said that unlocked it. She looks at her hands. The tears are there. She does not apologize for them.

He does not reach for her hand immediately. He gives her the room — because he understands, intuitively, the difference between presence and intrusion.

Then, carefully, he reaches across the table and places his hand over hers.

She does not pull away.

She is carrying her brother with her every day. She has been carrying him to school, to the library, to every future nursing lecture she will ever attend. He is the reason for everything she is building. He is invisible to the world around her, the way I was invisible. But he is the most present thing in her life. I understand this more than I expected to.

They sit like that for a while, her hand under his, the library quiet around them, the amber light doing what amber light does in libraries on Thursday afternoons.

She comes back to the library the following Thursday. She is already at the table when he arrives. She does not say anything about the previous week. Neither does he.

This is its own kind of fluency. They are becoming people who understand each other's silences.
