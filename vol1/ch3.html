Volume I Chapter Three

Academic Rise

Age 16 · Months 2–4
CLASSROOM 2-C · MONTH TWO

The first perfect quiz comes back on a Tuesday. Mrs. Andres does not announce it — she is not the announcing type — but she places it face-down on his desk with a particular economy of motion that is, for her, almost warm. He turns it over. 20/20. He looks at it for a moment, then looks up. The class is still receiving their own papers. No one is watching.

He writes in his notebook that evening: First 20/20. The system works. Refine, don't celebrate yet.

The top-three exam result comes six weeks later. His name appears on the list posted outside the teachers' lounge — third place, quarterly exam, Mathematics. He sees it on his way to the canteen during lunch and does not stop walking. He notes it. Files it. Keeps moving.

First place arrives in week ten. Integrated Science. He does not look at the posted list dramatically; he glances at it the way you glance at a clock when you already know roughly what time it is, confirms it, and continues to the library.

· · ·

The first oral recitation is the more significant event.

Mrs. Andres is reviewing the previous night's reading — the chapter on cell theory — and her eyes scan the room for a victim. They move past him in the usual way, arrive at Marcus, assess, move on, and then — perhaps because Marcus has the particular expression of someone who did not do the reading, perhaps because the universe has its own timing — her eyes come back.

"Aizawa."

The room does not go quiet dramatically. It goes quiet in the way rooms go quiet when something slightly unusual has happened — a low-frequency shift, a collective adjustment of attention.

He has practiced speaking aloud for six weeks. He has rehearsed every chapter's key concepts before going to bed. He has the particular quality of preparation that does not feel like performance because it has been done alone, for no audience, simply because he was building something.

He answers. His voice is even, unhurried, precise. He includes the detail he knows she'll want — the date of the theory's establishment, the two scientists, the three parts — and then he stops. Not because he ran out of words. Because that was the answer.

Mrs. Andres writes it on the board. She does not look at him when she says it, because she is already writing, but her voice has a quality it did not have in his direction before: "That's exactly right, Haruki."

His name. In her mouth. With weight behind it.

That's exactly right, Haruki. Seven syllables. She has said my name one hundred and twelve times on attendance rolls since second year began. This is the first time I heard it.
· · ·

The class begins to reconfigure around him in the way water reconfigures around a new stone in a stream — not dramatically, but inevitably.

Dana asks him to check her essay. He does. He gives specific feedback, not vague encouragement, and she looks at the annotations with the expression of someone who has just received useful information rather than comfortable noise. She thanks him. She tells Yumi. Yumi tells someone else.

The whisper passes through the social architecture of Classroom 2-C like a slow current: Kailan pa siya naging ganyan? When did he become like this?

Since always. You just weren't listening.
· · ·
THE MARCO MOMENT
Plot Twist #2 — The Ministry Connection

Marco Villanueva transfers to 2-C on a Wednesday in the third month. He is not the quiet kind of new student. He arrives the way certain people arrive — with the natural ease of someone who has never been uninteresting to a room, who understands instinctively that confidence is a kind of gift you give other people by demonstrating that social stakes are not as high as they fear.

Within two days, he knows everyone's name. Within four, he is in two social groups and has declined a third without anyone taking offense. He is the kind of person who makes a room feel more like itself.

On his fifth day, he sits behind Haruki.

Haruki is aware of him the way you are aware of weather — not anxiously, but as a fact of the environment. Marco has noticed his name on the exam list, has perhaps filed it. But they have not spoken.

Then Marco leans forward during morning break. He leans forward with the confidence of someone who is about to say something he has been waiting to say, and he says it in the particular register of a voice that carries, because some voices simply carry:

"Haruki! Ikaw yung nagpiano sa CICF youth conference noong Hunyo! Grabe ka dun, pre!"

The class goes quiet.

Not the polite quiet of a recitation. The specific quiet that happens when a room suddenly receives new information about someone it thought it knew — or thought it didn't know, which amounts to the same thing.

Every eye in Classroom 2-C turns to Seat 38.

Haruki turns to look at Marco. His expression is the thing that no one expected: completely calm. Not surprised, not flustered, not performing modesty. The entirely settled expression of a person who has been known somewhere else the whole time and is simply — being reminded of it.

"Oo," he says. Yes.

Marco grins. "Seryoso ka, ang galing mo, pare. Yung arrangement mo ng last song — grabe." Seriously, you were incredible. That arrangement of the last song — incredible.

"Salamat," Haruki says. And that is it.

But the room does not go back to what it was before. That is not how information works. The class has just learned that the boy in Seat 38 — who they knew only as a name on Mrs. Andres's roll and a perfect quiz score and a first-place on an exam list — was good enough at something that the new student, who knows everyone, came to school specifically knowing about it.

The crack in the invisibility is not dramatic. It is a single line, thin, running from corner to corner. But cracks work by geometry, not by drama.

Dana leans to Yumi and says, quietly enough that she thinks Haruki can't hear but loudly enough that he can: "Yung church piano guy pala siya." So he's the church piano guy.

Haruki faces forward. He writes the date at the top of a new page in his notebook. He writes the chapter heading for today's lesson.

He does not smile. But something in his expression, visible only to Marco from behind, is the very specific expression of someone who has been patient for a long time and is now simply waiting for everyone else to catch up.
