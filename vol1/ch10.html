Volume I — Finale Chapter Ten

Graduation Performance

Age 18 · June · The Last Night of High School
THE AUDITION · TWO WEEKS PRIOR

It is Marco who suggests the graduation program. He drops the signup sheet on both their desks on the same morning and says, with the particular cheerfulness of someone who has already decided how this story ends: "Piano at vocals. Obvious na kung sino-sino." Piano and vocals. Obvious who.

Haruki looks at Yuna. Yuna looks at Haruki. Neither of them says anything for a moment. Then she takes the sheet, writes her name in the vocals slot, pushes it to him. He writes his name in the piano slot. Neither of them makes it into more than it is — they are both, by now, people who understand the difference between what something means and what it requires to announce it.

They rehearse four times. The first rehearsal is technically competent and emotionally flat — the particular flatness of two people who are paying too much attention to being precise and not enough to being present. The second rehearsal is better. By the third, something shifts in the way things shift between people who trust each other's attention: they stop performing for each other and start simply — being in the music.

The fourth rehearsal is so good that they both go quiet afterward, not looking at each other, in the specific silence of people who have just done something they know they will remember.

· · ·
GRADUATION NIGHT · THE AUDITORIUM

They perform third in the program. She stands at the microphone with the composure of someone who does not need a microphone to be heard — her presence at the stand is natural, unhurried, the posture of someone who has stood before a congregation and sung.

He plays the introduction. She finds the melody the way she finds everything she looks for: directly, without search, as if it was already where she was going.

They play their graduation song.

It is synchronized not because they rehearsed it into precision but because they have been paying close attention to each other for two years. Two years of library Thursdays and church Sundays and a talent show and quiet conversations and one hand placed carefully over another hand on a table. Two years of building a language that does not require full sentences to be understood.

The auditorium, full of graduating seniors and their families, goes very still.

Teachers who have watched hundreds of graduation programs are watching this one differently. Mrs. Andres, who is not a demonstrative person, is leaning forward slightly. Marco, in the second row, has the expression of a person watching something they had a hand in and feel entirely undeserving of credit for.

The music fills the auditorium and goes past it, into the warm June air of the courtyard outside, where it becomes part of the night.

· · ·
THE COURTYARD · AFTER

They are outside afterward. The noise of the auditorium — families, phones, the particular organized chaos of an event ending — filters through the doors behind them. The courtyard is mostly empty. There is a lamp post. There is June air, warm, the smell of the flowers in the administrative building's planters. There are stars.

They stand in the quiet the way they have always stood in the quiet: fully, without needing to fill it.

"Yuna," he says.

"Mm."

"I think you already know what I want to say."

She looks at him. The lamp post light is behind them and the auditorium light is ahead and they are in the middle of both, in the particular kind of half-light that belongs to moments between what was and what will be.

Her look is the one he knows — assessing, already concluded, the look of someone whose internal process has been going on longer than its external expression.

"Oo," she says softly. Yes.

He does not confess dramatically. He does not perform the moment. He says:

"I want to keep showing up for you. At school. At church. Wherever the next thing is. Is that okay?"

She is quiet for a moment. She is looking at him with the particular attention of someone who is deciding whether to say the thing they have been holding.

Then she says: "Haruki — I was there the first Sunday you played. Before all of this. Before school noticed you. I saw you then."

He goes very still.

"I didn't know," he says.

"I know you didn't." She smiles — the first full smile he has seen on her, the kind that does not preface itself or apologize. "That's why I stayed."

· · ·

She chose him before his glow-up. She came to the church before the talent show, before the exam scores, before the chess tournament, before Marco's announcement in Classroom 2-C. She sat in the third row and watched a boy play piano for God when no one at school was watching, and she saw something worth staying for.

She stayed because of who he already was. Not who he was becoming.

He holds this. He holds it the way he held Lola Perla's nod, the way he held the pastor's challenge in the sound room — fully, with the understanding that some things are given only once and must be kept for the rest of your life.

Some people see you before the world does.
Keep those people.

The auditorium noise behind them has settled into families gathering children and taking photographs and looking for bags. The June night does what June nights do — warm, full, unhurried. The lamp post continues its work.

They walk back in together. Not holding hands yet — that will come, because things come in their time, and they are both people who understand that. But side by side, with the particular quality of two people who have arrived at something they have been moving toward for two years and do not need to announce it.

High school ends. Volume I ends. The piano, in a church two jeepneys north, waits for the next Sunday.

It always waits.
