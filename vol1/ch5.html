Volume I Chapter Five

Social Shift

Age 16–17 · Month 5
CLASSROOM 2-C · MONTH FIVE

The classroom rearranges itself around him the way a city rearranges around a new structure — not dramatically, not all at once, but with the quiet inevitability of things that have actually changed. Groups that once formed around other people now form partly around him. Project assignments acquire a particular gravitational quality: si Haruki na lang. Let's just get Haruki.

He leads without performing leadership. This is the distinction that matters. When a study group forms and looks to him for direction, he does not step forward with the self-conscious energy of someone claiming something. He simply begins — organizes the material, divides the sections, identifies who is strongest at what and assigns accordingly. He speaks in the quiet, certain tone of someone who has led a worship set in front of a congregation and knows that the most important thing a leader can do is make everyone around them feel that the work is worth doing.

Public presence is not new to him. Only this audience is.

· · ·

Yumi draws stars on the corner of his notebook during homeroom one morning, the small unconscious way of marking affection that people do when they feel comfortable around someone. He doesn't say anything. He lets the stars be there.

Pia, during a break, asks: "Nagpipiano ka ba talaga? Totoo ba?" Do you really play piano? Is that true?

"Totoo," he says.

"Anong klase ng kanta? Classical ba?" What kind of songs? Classical?

"Lahat. Worship music mostly. At minsan original compositions." Everything. Worship music mostly. And sometimes original compositions.

"Sana marinig ko someday." I hope I can hear you play someday.

"Meron sa church, Linggo-Linggo," he says. There's one at church every Sunday.

Pia laughs — lightly, not unkindly, the laugh of someone who has just encountered an answer that was simpler and more direct than they expected. It is easy and uncomplicated, and Haruki finds he does not need to make it more than it is.

· · ·
Plot Twist #4 — Yuna Observes Him at Church

On a Sunday morning in June, Yuna Takahashi attends his church for the first time. She does not announce this to anyone. She comes with her aunt — Tita Rosario, who has been a member for eleven years and who had mentioned the church's worship team so many times over dinner that Yuna finally agreed to come once, to put the question to rest.

She sits in the third row. She folds her hands in her lap. The service begins.

Haruki is at the piano when she comes in. He does not see her enter. He is mid-worship set, into the second song, eyes partially closed in that particular way he has when he is both playing and praying — present to the room and present to something beyond the room simultaneously, the way musicians are who understand that what they do is not performance but offering.

Yuna watches him.

She recognizes him immediately — the boy from Seat 38, the quiet one who corrected her textbook edition in the library, who returned her novel without drama, whose exam scores are now the ones the rest of the class compares themselves against. She has noticed him in the way you notice something that keeps being where you didn't expect it.

But here — here he is completely different.

Not louder. Not more outgoing. More present. Like a signal that has finally found its receiver. Like someone whose frequency you have been missing because you were listening on the wrong band, and now the right band is on and you hear it and you wonder how it was never audible before.

He plays through the service. He plays the way someone plays when they mean every note and do not need to prove it. He leads the congregation through the worship set with the settled authority of someone who does this because it is who he is — not a performance of devotion but the actual thing.

She stays for the entire service. She does not say anything to anyone, including her aunt, about what she is thinking.

But she thinks about it for one full week before she speaks to him at school.

She was there. She was in the third row and I did not see her. The thing I do when no one is watching — she was watching. I don't know yet that this matters. I will.
· · ·

The social geography of 2-C continues its slow reorganization. He is not the center of it — he does not need to be the center of things, and this quality of not needing centrality is itself a kind of magnetic force. People are drawn to those who do not require their attention, who give it freely without demanding its return.

He sits with different groups at lunch. He does not attach himself permanently to any of them. He moves through the social landscape of his class the way he moves through music — present in each moment, not trying to sustain it past its natural end.

Marco, who caused the first crack in the invisibility, has become something like a friend — not a best friend in the confessional sense, but the kind of friend who makes things easier by existing nearby, who shares a faith and a humor and a willingness to do things without explaining themselves too much.

"Pre, nag-notice na sila sa'yo," Marco says one afternoon, watching Haruki pack his bag. Man, they're noticing you now.

Haruki zips his bag. "Sabi mo." You say so.

"'Di ba masaya?" Isn't that good?

Haruki thinks about it genuinely. He thinks about Lola Perla's nod. He thinks about the pastor's flame emoji. He thinks about the particular silence of a room after music ends well.

"Mas masaya kung tama ang dahilan," he says. It's better when the reason is right.

Marco looks at him for a moment. Then nods slowly, the nod of someone who knows exactly what that means.

They walk to the gate together. The afternoon light is gold on the concrete. Haruki takes the jeepney north, toward the church, toward the piano, toward the frequency that has always been on.
