Volume I Chapter Two

The Decision to Change

Age 16 · Tuesday, 11 PM
11:07 PM · TUESDAY

It is not a dramatic night. There is no inciting incident, no humiliation he must overcome, no rival who looked at him the wrong way and ignited something. It is Tuesday. He is at his desk. He has finished his homework and has picked up his Bible the way he does most evenings, not from obligation but from habit — the good kind of habit, the kind that has worn a groove in the wood of his days.

He is in Proverbs. He reads slowly, which is how he reads everything, the way he listens to music — not to get through it but to let it arrive at its own pace. He reads through chapter twenty, and then twenty-one, and he arrives at verse five and stops.

"The plans of the diligent lead to profit as surely as haste leads to poverty." Proverbs 21:5

He reads it again. Then again. He puts the Bible down on his desk, not closed, open to that page, and he sits with it the way you sit with something that has just said your name in a room where you didn't expect to hear it.

The servant who buried his talent. He has thought about this parable before — it is not unfamiliar territory, Pastor Reyes has preached on it twice in the years Haruki has been attending the church. But there is a difference between hearing a parable and hearing it about yourself. Tonight, for the first time, it lands with the specific weight of a thing that is aimed.

I have been burying the talent. Not the piano — I know I am using that. But the rest. The mind. The discipline. The attention that I give to music and have been giving to nothing else. I have been sitting in Seat 38 like I was assigned to invisibility. Like it was a fact of the world instead of a choice I am making every day by choosing nothing.

He closes his eyes. He is not performing this moment for anyone. There is no one here. It is 11 PM on a Tuesday and his parents are asleep and the city outside his window is doing the city's nighttime thing, cars and distant voices and the occasional jeepney horn. None of this is watching him.

He opens the drawer of his desk and takes out a blank notebook — not the school notebook, not the music notation book, a clean one his mother bought three months ago and he hadn't found a purpose for. He opens it to the first page.

He writes, in his careful handwriting: Day 1.

Below it, he writes: The decision is not to impress anyone. The decision is because I believe I was given something I've been sitting on.

He closes the notebook. He puts it in a specific place in the drawer where it will be the first thing he sees tomorrow morning. He gets ready for bed. He turns off the light.

The city continues doing its nighttime thing. He listens to it for a while, not anxiously, just present, and then he falls asleep.

· · ·

What changes is structural, not theatrical. He does not arrive at school the next day transformed — his hair is the same, his uniform is pressed the same way it always is, Mrs. Andres still calls roll and her eyes still move past him. The outside world does not know anything has happened.

But his notebook is now color-coded. Blue for information he needs to understand deeply. Green for information he needs to retain. Red for things he's gotten wrong and needs to revisit. He does this quietly, at Seat 38, during the first break while other students are doing whatever they do during breaks. He is not making a statement. He is making a system.

His posture changes by the third day. Not dramatically — he does not suddenly sit ramrod-straight like a military cadet. But he occupies his space differently. His spine is upright rather than curved. His feet are on the floor. He takes up the actual physical space his body is allowed and does not apologize for it by hunching. It is a small thing. He notices it in himself and decides to keep it.

He practices speaking aloud. Not in class — not yet, not immediately — but in his room, at night, after piano practice. He reads his notes out loud to himself. He summarizes what he learned that day, in full sentences, without looking at his books. He does this for fifteen minutes before bed. He sounds strange to himself at first, the way you always sound strange to yourself when you begin practicing a thing.

He studies the top students the way a chess player studies board positions. Not their notes — their attention. Where Angelica looks when she's listening. How quickly Marcus processes a new formula. The particular quality of stillness that Reyes — class president, academic standout — maintains during a lecture. These are things that can be observed and learned.

· · ·

His mother hears the piano at six AM on a Thursday morning and thinks he is practicing for church. She does not disturb him. She makes coffee in the kitchen and listens to the sound of her son playing scales, which she has been listening to since he was ten, and feels the particular contentment of a parent whose child is doing something purposeful in the early morning when they could be sleeping.

He is not practicing for church. He is playing because music is the one place he has always been fully present, and he is trying to import that quality of presence into other areas of his life. He is using the piano the way a runner uses a warmup — to remind his body and mind what full engagement feels like, so he can carry it to the desk, to the classroom, to the version of himself he is building.

· · ·
CLASSROOM 2-C · WEEK THREE

The book falls from the desk at the front of the room on a Wednesday morning. He is the closest person to it. He is also the only person who notices it fall — the students nearby are facing forward, listening to Mrs. Andres's explanation of the next assignment.

He stands. He walks to the front row, third seat from the window. He picks up the book. It is a novel — he registers this, the particular texture of fiction in his hand. He places it back on the desk.

The girl looks up. She has dark hair pulled back and dark eyes that assess things very quickly and a quality of attention that is, he realizes in the 1.5 seconds of eye contact, very similar to the quality of attention he has been observing in the top students. She has been paying attention this whole time. She just hasn't made it visible.

"Salamat," she says.

He nods.

He returns to Seat 38. He does not know her name. He will think about those 1.5 seconds for the next three days, not in a way that disrupts him, but in the way that certain things stay present in the mind because they are quietly significant and the mind knows it even when the person doesn't yet.

She said salamat with the particular intonation of someone who meant it. Not reflex. Actual. She registered that I did something. That is more than most people do in a day.

He writes in his notebook that evening — not about her, about the system. He notes what worked that day, what didn't. He adjusts the color-coding. He reads Proverbs 21:5 again, not because he has forgotten it, but because some things need to be read repeatedly before they become structural rather than inspirational.

He writes: The decision is spiritual before it is practical. I am not doing this to become something for other people to see. I am doing this because I was given something and I am going to use it.

He closes the notebook. Five-forty AM tomorrow. Piano first. Then everything else.

He sleeps.
