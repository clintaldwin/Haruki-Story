Volume I Chapter Nine

Connection Deepens

Age 17 · Church and School
SUNDAY MORNING · THE CHURCH

By November, Yuna and Haruki attend the same church regularly. This is not a formal arrangement — no one has said we are doing this now, no conversation has named it. She comes with her Tita Rosario. He was already there. These facts have simply, over the course of several weeks, become adjacent and then overlapping.

The worship team meets them as a pair without making anything of it, in the way of people who have known Haruki long enough to understand that the things in his life arrive quietly and mean more than they announce. Ate Mila offers Yuna the alto section of the choir — "Parang may boses ka, anak" — and Yuna, who does not typically join things easily, says yes. This surprises her.

Lola Perla — the eighty-one-year-old woman who nodded at Haruki during a prayer meeting in the story's first week, the nod that held him through several subsequent days of school — finds Yuna after the service one Sunday morning. She takes Yuna's hands in hers with the particular grip of a very old person who has decided that physical warmth is no longer something to be modest about.

"Ang bata na ito," she says, nodding toward Haruki across the sanctuary, where he is coiling cables with careful hands. "Matagal na siyang kumakanta para sa Diyos kahit walang nakarinig."

This young man — he has been singing for God for a long time, even when no one was listening.

Yuna watches him across the sanctuary. He does not know she is watching. He is setting up the music stand for the next service with the focused economy of someone who does things carefully because he believes that care is a form of respect, not just a technique.

She holds Lola Perla's words the way she holds information she considers important: precisely, without sentimentality, turning it in her mind to understand its shape.

She was there the first Sunday I played. She is here now, in the third row, in the choir section. She has been coming back every Sunday for three months. I don't know she heard what Lola Perla said to her. I don't know she is watching me set up the music stand. I am just setting up the music stand.

THE FIRST TIME YUNA SINGS

She joins the choir for the first time on a Sunday in December. She has been attending the practices — Ate Mila runs them on Saturday mornings, gentle and organized — and has been quiet in them, learning the harmonies the way she learns everything: carefully, completely, without performance.

Haruki is at the piano. He is into the second song of the worship set, his hands doing what they always do — the music moving through him in the particular way it does when he is both playing and praying, the two states he cannot fully separate after years of practice. He is not listening for individuals in the choir. He is listening for the room.

And then he hears her.

Not loudly. She is not performing. She is singing the alto line with the particular quality of someone who means what they are saying and does not need anyone else to know they mean it. Her voice is clear, slightly imperfect in the way that voices are imperfect when they are actually human rather than technically correct — the specific imperfection that makes something more honest than flawless.

It is exactly the way she is.

His hands almost falter. Not because he misses a note — he doesn't — but because there is a moment where the external and internal are too close together and the hands that hold both briefly register the proximity. He recovers in a beat and plays on and no one notices.

But he notices. He holds the sound of her voice the way he held Lola Perla's nod the previous year — as something that will sustain him for longer than its moment.

Plot Twist #7 — Yuna's Mother Disapproves

She tells him on a Thursday in the library. She tells him the way she tells him everything — directly, without excessive setup, with the assessment already complete before she speaks.

"Ayaw ng mama ko sa church," she says. "Sa akin talaga — ayaw niya sa lahat ng nangyayari sa church at sa mga taong nakakakilala ko doon."

My mom doesn't like the church. Me specifically — she doesn't like everything that's happening at the church and the people I'm meeting there.

He waits.

"Ikaw specifically," she says. "Sabi niya: 'Estudyante lang siya. Wala pa siyang mapapakita.'"

You specifically. She says: 'He's just a student. He has nothing to show yet.'

There is a silence. He processes this with the stillness of someone who has learned to receive difficult information and not let it immediately become something he has to manage.

Then: "Tama siya," he says.

She looks at him.

"Estudyante ako. Wala pa talaga akong mapapakita — hindi pa. Pero may plano ako." He pauses. "Hindi para i-impress siya. Pero dahil karapat-dapat siyang makita ang plano, hindi lang ang tao."

She's right. I'm just a student. I truly don't have anything to show yet — not yet. But I have a plan. Not to impress her. But because she deserves to see the plan, not just the person.

This is the first time he has said this out loud to anyone. The plan — the blueprint in the notebook, the disciplines of the 5 AM mornings, the architecture he has been building since a Tuesday night and Proverbs 21:5. He has held it privately because it was private, because things under construction do not need an audience, but now he is saying it: there is a plan.

Yuna looks at him for a long moment. She is not easily moved by words — she values action, specificity, the demonstrated thing. But she knows the particular quality of a person who means what they say, who has made a decision that preceded the sentence that articulates it.

"Ilang taon mo nang pinaplano?" she asks. How long have you been planning it?

"Isang taon at kalahati." A year and a half.

She nods once. Files it. Goes back to her textbook.

He goes back to his notes. The library is quiet and amber around them. Outside, the afternoon is doing its afternoon thing, indifferent and full.

I have been asked to prove myself to someone I have not met, for the sake of someone who has already decided to stay. The strange thing is that this does not feel like pressure. It feels like a direction.
